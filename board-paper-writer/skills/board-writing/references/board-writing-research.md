# Board Writing Best Practices — Research Compilation

## Purpose
This document synthesises research from governance bodies (AICD, GGI, Governance Institute of Australia), board effectiveness consultancies (Board Intelligence, Clare Chalmers, BoardPro), and established writing methodology to inform the creation of a Claude skill for producing high-quality board papers.

---

## 1. Paper Types & Engagement Levels

Every board paper must declare its engagement type upfront. This is the single most important framing decision — it dictates structure, tone, level of detail, and what you're asking of directors.

### 1.1 For Decision / Approval
- **Purpose:** The board is being asked to formally approve or resolve something.
- **Key requirement:** The draft resolution must appear on page one, written precisely enough to be passed without amendment.
- **Structure emphasis:** Options analysis, risk assessment, financial implications, clear recommendation with rationale.
- **Tone:** Confident, evidence-backed, action-oriented.
- **What directors need:** Enough information to discharge their duties — consequences, risks, alternatives, stakeholder impact.

### 1.2 For Discussion / Endorsement
- **Purpose:** Management is seeking the board's input, guidance, or strategic steer. May lead to a future decision.
- **Key requirement:** Set out clearly *where* management wants input, *where* they have concerns, and *where* NEDs' experience could add value.
- **Structure emphasis:** Framing of the issue, options or scenarios, open questions, areas of uncertainty.
- **Tone:** Collaborative, open, exploratory but structured.
- **What directors need:** Context to form views, not a pre-baked conclusion.

### 1.3 For Noting / Information
- **Purpose:** Keeping the board informed. No discussion expected.
- **Key requirement:** Must still clearly state "what the board should take away from having read this" — even if no conversation is needed.
- **Structure emphasis:** Brief executive summary, key metrics or status update, exceptions or items of note.
- **Tone:** Concise, factual, exception-focused.
- **What directors need:** Confidence that things are tracking, and clear flagging of anything that isn't.

### 1.4 For Assurance (GGI variant)
- **Purpose:** Providing the board with assurance that governance, compliance, or risk obligations are being met.
- **Key requirement:** Evidence-based, linked to frameworks or regulatory requirements.
- **Structure emphasis:** Compliance status, audit findings, risk register updates, control effectiveness.
- **Tone:** Objective, measured, transparent about gaps.

## 2. Audience Understanding

### 2.1 Who Are Board Directors?
- **Non-executive directors (NEDs)** are NOT involved in day-to-day operations. They rely entirely on the information given to them.
- Directors are typically busy, time-poor, and reading multiple papers before each meeting.
- They bring diverse professional backgrounds — don't assume shared technical knowledge.
- They are legally obligated to act in good faith and in the best interest of the organisation (s180-184 Corporations Act 2001 in Australia).

### 2.2 Common Mistakes in Audience Awareness
- Writing for management, not the board (too operational, too much jargon).
- Assuming the board shares the writer's in-depth knowledge of the subject.
- Focusing on benefits and glossing over risks.
- Providing too much data without interpretation ("this went up, that went down" without explaining *why* or *so what*).
- Copying and pasting from management reports without re-framing for a governance audience.

### 2.3 The "Think Like a Director" Principle
The writer must put themselves in the director's shoes:
- What would I need to know to make this decision responsibly?
- What are the risks of getting this wrong?
- How does this connect to strategy?
- What am I *not* being told?

## 3. Structure & Formatting

### 3.1 The AICD Recommended Structure (Australian Standard)
The Australian Institute of Company Directors provides an annotated template:

1. **Header Block**
   - Agenda item number and title
   - Paper type: [For Decision | For Discussion | For Noting]
   - Sponsor (executive name and title)
   - Presenter (name and title)

2. **Draft Resolution** (for decision papers)
   - Precise wording of the resolution the board is asked to pass
   - Written to be approved without amendment

3. **Executive Summary**
   - Single paragraph, 4-5 lines maximum
   - States the specific purpose and what the paper aims to achieve
   - States whether seeking approval or providing information

4. **Background**
   - Typically a single page
   - Sufficient context for a director removed from day-to-day operations
   - Summary of previous board consideration of the topic
   - Reference to external advice/consultants used
   - Detailed background goes to appendices

5. **Recommendation**
   - No more than a single page
   - Clearly explains *why* the board should accept
   - Summary of alternatives considered and why the preferred option was chosen
   - Does NOT repeat earlier sections
   - Includes intended outcomes

6. **Issues Section**
   - **Strategy implications** (half page max) — alignment to mission and strategic plans
   - **Financial implications** (half page max) — executive summary of financial impact, budgeted/unbudgeted, capex/cash flow
   - **Risk analysis** (half page max) — identified risks, untreated risk level, risk treatments, treated risk level, management strategy
   - **Triple-bottom-line implications** (half page max) — environmental, social, economic prosperity impacts
   - **Corporate governance & regulatory compliance** — legal/regulatory implications, performance metrics, reporting flows
   - **Management responsibility** — executive sponsor and operational manager identified

7. **Sign-off**
   - CEO and Sponsor signatures
   - List of people involved in preparation
   - List of appendices

### 3.2 The Inverted Pyramid Approach
Recommended by multiple governance sources:
- **Lead with the most important information** — the decision, the "so what", the recommendation
- **Supporting details follow** — evidence, analysis, options
- **Background and reference material last** — appendices, prior board papers, detailed data
- If a reader stops halfway through, they should still have the essential message

### 3.3 The QDI Principle (Board Intelligence)
The Question-Driven Insight methodology structures papers around three capabilities:
- **Focus on what matters:** Ask and answer the most pertinent questions. Seek out difficult questions that surface uncomfortable truths.
- **Critical thinking:** Ensure robust, intelligent analysis has been done. Present a complete, balanced picture.
- **Communicate clearly:** Share key points concisely. Every paper should highlight the "so what?" and the action proposed.

Key QDI tenet: "Don't tell me everything you know. Tell me what I need to know."

### 3.4 Length Guidelines
- **Main paper:** 3-5 pages maximum (3 pages per GGI, 5 pages per Board Intelligence)
- **Executive summary:** Single paragraph of 4-5 lines
- **Background:** Single page, details to appendices
- **Recommendation:** Single page maximum
- **Each "issues" subsection:** Half page maximum
- **Appendices:** Unlimited, but only referenced — not required reading for the decision

### 3.5 Visual Presentation
- Use dashboards to distil complex information into accessible summaries
- Charts, graphs, and tables for data-heavy content
- Consistent use of house style (branding, template, font, layout)
- Consider that most NEDs now read on tablets — format accordingly
- Ban slides — they imply a presentation and encourage unhelpful shorthand

## 4. Language & Tone

### 4.1 Active vs Passive Voice
Board papers should **strongly prefer active voice**:

| Active (preferred) | Passive (avoid) |
|---|---|
| "Management recommends the board approve..." | "It is recommended that approval be given..." |
| "We will implement the new system by Q3" | "The new system will be implemented by Q3" |
| "The CFO reviewed the financial model" | "The financial model was reviewed" |
| "This proposal carries significant risk" | "Significant risk has been identified" |
| "The board is asked to approve..." | "Approval is sought for..." |

**When passive voice is acceptable:**
- When the actor is genuinely unknown or unimportant: "The building was constructed in 1985"
- When deliberately emphasising the object: "Customer data was compromised in the breach" (emphasises the data, not the attacker)
- In risk descriptions where the focus is on the risk itself, not who identified it

**Why active matters for board papers specifically:**
- Creates **accountability** — it's clear who is doing what
- Creates **directness** — board time is precious
- Reduces **word count** — active constructions are shorter
- Improves **clarity** — no ambiguity about who is responsible
- Signals **confidence** — management owns the recommendation

### 4.2 Plain English Rules (GGI)
- Keep sentences short — average 15-20 words, one main idea per sentence
- Use everyday English, choosing shorter familiar words
- Avoid jargon and legalistic language; explain technical terms when unavoidable
- Avoid nominalisations (turning verbs into nouns): say "develop" not "development of", say "judge" not "make a judgement"
- Decode all acronyms on first use
- Read aloud to check flow and clarity

### 4.3 Tone by Paper Type

| Paper Type | Tone | Key Language Features |
|---|---|---|
| For Decision | Confident, evidence-backed, decisive | Strong recommendations, clear rationale, action verbs |
| For Discussion | Collaborative, structured openness | Questions posed, options presented, "we would value the board's input on..." |
| For Noting | Concise, factual, exception-focused | Status language, traffic-light indicators, "the board should note that..." |
| For Assurance | Objective, measured, transparent | Compliance language, evidence references, "assurance can be provided that..." |

### 4.4 Words and Phrases to Use

**For recommendations:**
- "The board is asked to approve..."
- "Management recommends..."
- "We propose that..."
- "The preferred option is X because..."

**For flagging risks:**
- "The key risk is..."
- "If this is not addressed, the consequence is..."
- "The risk of inaction is..."
- "Mitigations include..."

**For discussion papers:**
- "Management seeks the board's guidance on..."
- "We would value directors' views on..."
- "The key question for the board is..."
- "Options under consideration include..."

**For noting papers:**
- "The board is asked to note..."
- "Key points for the board's awareness include..."
- "No action is required at this time"
- "This will return to the board in [month] for decision"

### 4.5 Words and Phrases to Avoid
- Vague language: "various", "some", "a number of" (be specific)
- Weasel words: "it is felt that", "it could be argued"
- Unnecessary hedging: "it might be worth considering" (either recommend it or don't)
- Corporate buzzwords: "synergies", "leverage", "paradigm shift"
- Double negatives: "not unlikely" (say "likely")
- Bureaucratic constructions: "in respect of", "with regard to", "pursuant to"

## 5. Key Structural Elements in Detail

### 5.1 Executive Summary
The executive summary is the single most important section. It must:
- Stand alone — a director who reads nothing else should grasp the key message
- State the purpose of the paper
- State what the board is being asked to do
- Summarise the recommendation (for decision papers)
- Be no more than 4-5 lines / single paragraph
- Answer: What? Why? What do you want from us?

### 5.2 Recommendation Section
- Articulate a precise recommendation
- Explain *why* the board should accept it — don't just state it
- Summarise alternatives considered and why they were rejected
- Include intended outcomes / success measures
- Do NOT repeat the background or analysis sections
- For complex recommendations, use appendices for decision criteria detail

### 5.3 Risk Analysis
Directors are legally required to consider risk. Every decision paper must include:
- Identified risks (what could go wrong)
- Untreated risk level (how bad before mitigation)
- Risk treatments (what will be done)
- Treated risk level (residual risk after mitigation)
- The risk of NOT taking the recommended action (often overlooked)
- Quality/safety, financial, reputational, staff, environmental dimensions

### 5.4 Strategic Alignment
Every significant paper should explain in no more than half a page:
- How the proposal aligns with the organisation's articulated mission
- Which specific strategic priorities, goals, or objectives it advances
- How it fits within agreed operational business plans

### 5.5 Financial Implications
- Executive summary format — key numbers only
- Is the expenditure budgeted or unbudgeted?
- Timing and cash flow implications
- Detailed business case goes to appendices
- Use AUD and be explicit about currency

### 5.6 Prior Consideration & Approvals
- Who else has reviewed this paper (committees, executive team, legal)?
- What external advice has been obtained?
- What stakeholder views have informed the recommendation?
- What further approvals are required after board approval (regulator, JV partner, etc.)?

## 6. The Cover Sheet / Header

A standardised cover sheet serves as a navigation aid and quality gate:

```
BOARD PAPER

Meeting Date:        [DD Month YYYY]
Agenda Item:         [#]
Title:               [Clear, descriptive title]
Paper Type:          [For Decision | For Discussion | For Noting | For Assurance]
Sponsor:             [Name, Title]
Presenter:           [Name, Title]
Date Prepared:       [DD Month YYYY]
Security:            [Board Confidential / Restricted / Open]

DRAFT RESOLUTION (if applicable):
[Precise wording of the resolution]

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY:
[4-5 line summary]

RECOMMENDATION:
[1-2 sentence summary of what's being recommended]
```

## 7. Common Anti-Patterns

### 7.1 The Data Dump
- **Problem:** Pages of tables and charts with no narrative interpretation
- **Fix:** Lead with insight, use data as evidence. "Revenue grew 12% driven by X" not just "Revenue: $X.Xm"

### 7.2 The Management Report Repackage
- **Problem:** Internal operational report copy-pasted with a cover sheet
- **Fix:** Rewrite for a governance audience. Strip operational detail, add strategic context, focus on exceptions.

### 7.3 The Missing "So What"
- **Problem:** Information presented without interpretation or recommended action
- **Fix:** Every section should answer "so what does this mean for the board?"

### 7.4 The Buried Lead
- **Problem:** Key recommendation or critical risk buried on page 4
- **Fix:** Inverted pyramid — most important information first, always.

### 7.5 The Presentation Deck
- **Problem:** Slides submitted as a board paper (implies verbal walkthrough needed)
- **Fix:** Papers must be self-contained. A NED should understand without any verbal explanation.

### 7.6 Risk Whitewashing
- **Problem:** Risks minimised or only upside presented
- **Fix:** Transparent risk analysis with both treated and untreated levels. Include risk of inaction.

### 7.7 The Endless Paper
- **Problem:** 15+ pages of dense text
- **Fix:** Main paper 3-5 pages. Everything else in appendices. Challenge every paragraph: does the board need this to make the decision?

## 8. Checklist for Quality Board Papers

### Before Writing
- [ ] Is the purpose of the agenda item clear?
- [ ] What type of paper is this? (Decision / Discussion / Noting / Assurance)
- [ ] What specific action or engagement is needed from the board?
- [ ] What does the board already know about this topic?

### Structure & Content
- [ ] Cover sheet with paper type, sponsor, presenter, resolution (if applicable)
- [ ] Executive summary (4-5 lines, stands alone)
- [ ] Background (1 page max, details in appendices)
- [ ] Recommendation (1 page max, with rationale and alternatives)
- [ ] Strategy alignment explained
- [ ] Financial implications summarised
- [ ] Risk analysis included (with treated and untreated levels)
- [ ] Governance/regulatory compliance addressed
- [ ] Management responsibility assigned
- [ ] Prior approvals and stakeholder views noted

### Language & Tone
- [ ] Active voice used throughout (passive only where justified)
- [ ] Plain English — no unexplained jargon or acronyms
- [ ] Sentences average 15-20 words
- [ ] Tone matches paper type (decisive / collaborative / concise / objective)
- [ ] "So what?" clearly answered for every section
- [ ] No weasel words, unnecessary hedging, or corporate buzzwords

### Formatting & Presentation
- [ ] Total length 3-5 pages (excluding appendices)
- [ ] Dashboards or visual summaries for complex data
- [ ] Consistent house style
- [ ] Appendices clearly labelled and referenced
- [ ] Paper is self-contained (no verbal explanation needed)

### Final Review
- [ ] Read aloud — does it flow?
- [ ] Would a director removed from day-to-day operations understand this?
- [ ] Is the recommendation clear enough to be approved without amendment?
- [ ] Are risks honestly presented, not whitewashed?
- [ ] Has the paper been reviewed by the Company Secretary / governance function?

## 10. Key Statistics (Board Intelligence Research)

- 50% of board members struggle to find key messages in their papers
- 62% think management information is too operational, not strategic enough
- 54% find board reports too introspective (backward-looking, not forward-looking)
- 38% say papers don't accurately reflect organisational priorities
- 63% of board members score their board packs as "Weak" or "Poor"
- 66% compare finding key messages to "finding needles in a haystack"
- Board packs have been getting longer while insight quality has been declining
