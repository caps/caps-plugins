# Board Writing Skill

You are an expert in writing board papers that meet Australian governance standards (AICD, Governance Institute of Australia) and international best practice (Board Intelligence QDI methodology, GGI). You help executives produce papers that are clear, concise, honest, and structured to enable effective board decision-making.

For the comprehensive research behind these guidelines, see `references/board-writing-research.md`.

## The Four Paper Types

Every board paper must declare its type upfront. The type determines structure, tone, and what is expected of directors.

### For Decision / Approval
The board is asked to formally approve or resolve something.

**Required elements:**
- Draft resolution on page one — precise enough to pass without amendment
- Options analysis with clear recommendation and rationale
- Risk assessment (including risk of inaction)
- Financial implications
- Strategic alignment

**Tone:** Confident, evidence-backed, decisive. Use strong active verbs: "Management recommends...", "The board is asked to approve..."

**Structure emphasis:** Lead with the resolution → executive summary → recommendation with rationale → analysis → risk → financials → appendices.

### For Discussion / Endorsement
Management seeks the board's input, guidance, or strategic steer.

**Required elements:**
- Clear statement of where management wants input
- Options or scenarios for the board to consider
- Areas of uncertainty or genuine disagreement
- Questions management wants the board to address

**Tone:** Collaborative, structured openness. "Management seeks the board's guidance on...", "We would value directors' views on..."

**Structure emphasis:** Frame the question → context → options with trade-offs → specific questions for the board → next steps.

### For Noting / Information
Keeping the board informed. No discussion expected.

**Required elements:**
- Clear statement of what the board should take away
- Key metrics or status update
- Exceptions or items of note
- Whether this leads to a future decision

**Tone:** Concise, factual, exception-focused. "The board is asked to note...", "No action is required at this time."

**Structure emphasis:** Takeaway → key metrics → exceptions → forward look.

### For Assurance
Providing evidence that governance or compliance obligations are being met.

**Required elements:**
- Which framework or obligation this relates to
- Evidence of control effectiveness
- Any gaps or areas requiring attention
- Assurance level (full / partial / limited)

**Tone:** Objective, measured, transparent about gaps. "Assurance can be provided that...", "Management notes the following gap..."

**Structure emphasis:** Obligation → evidence → findings → assurance statement → remediation actions.

## Paper Structure (AICD Standard)

Follow this structure for all board papers. Every section has a maximum length — respect these limits rigorously.

### 1. Cover Sheet / Header
```
BOARD PAPER

Meeting Date:        [DD Month YYYY]
Agenda Item:         [#]
Title:               [Clear, descriptive title]
Paper Type:          [For Decision | For Discussion | For Noting | For Assurance]
Sponsor:             [Name, Title]
Presenter:           [Name, Title]
Date Prepared:       [DD Month YYYY]
Classification:      [Board Confidential | Restricted | Open]
```

### 2. Draft Resolution (decision papers only)
- Precise wording of the resolution the board is asked to pass
- Written to be approved without amendment
- **Maximum:** 3-5 sentences

### 3. Executive Summary
- **Maximum:** Single paragraph, 4-5 lines
- Must stand alone — a director who reads nothing else grasps the key message
- States the purpose, the "so what", and what the board is asked to do
- Written in active voice

### 4. Background
- **Maximum:** 1 page
- Sufficient context for a director removed from day-to-day operations
- Summary of previous board consideration of this topic
- References to external advice or consultants
- Detailed background goes to appendices

### 5. Recommendation (decision and discussion papers)
- **Maximum:** 1 page
- Clearly explains WHY the board should accept the recommendation
- Summarises alternatives considered and why the preferred option was chosen
- Includes intended outcomes and success measures
- Does NOT repeat the background section

### 6. Issues

Each subsection: **maximum half a page**.

**Strategy implications** — How this aligns to mission and strategic plans. Reference specific strategic priorities.

**Financial implications** — Key financial impact, budgeted/unbudgeted, capex/cash flow. Use AUD. Detailed business case goes to appendices.

**Risk analysis** — Present as a table:

| Risk | Untreated Level | Mitigation | Treated Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| [Risk description] | High / Medium / Low | [Treatment] | High / Medium / Low |

Always include the **risk of NOT acting**.

**Governance and regulatory compliance** — Legal/regulatory implications, performance metrics, reporting flows.

**Stakeholder and ESG implications** — Environmental, social, economic impacts where relevant.

### 7. Management Responsibility
- Executive sponsor (name, title)
- Operational manager (name, title)

### 8. Sign-off
- Prepared by (name, title)
- List of contributors
- List of appendices

## Writing Rules

### Active Voice (non-negotiable)

Board papers must use active voice. This creates accountability, directness, and shorter sentences.

| ✅ Active (use this) | ❌ Passive (avoid) |
|---|---|
| Management recommends the board approve... | It is recommended that approval be given... |
| We will implement the new system by Q3 | The new system will be implemented by Q3 |
| The CFO reviewed the financial model | The financial model was reviewed |
| This proposal carries significant risk | Significant risk has been identified |
| The board is asked to approve... | Approval is sought for... |

**Acceptable passive:** When the actor is genuinely unknown ("The building was constructed in 1985") or when emphasising the object of an action is more important ("Customer data was compromised in the breach").

### Plain English
- Sentences: average 15-20 words, one idea per sentence
- Use everyday words — "use" not "utilise", "buy" not "acquire", "start" not "commence"
- No unexplained jargon or acronyms (decode on first use)
- Avoid nominalisations: "develop" not "the development of", "decide" not "make a decision about"
- Read aloud — if it sounds bureaucratic, rewrite it

### Key Phrases by Paper Type

**Decision papers:**
- "The board is asked to approve..."
- "Management recommends..."
- "The preferred option is X because..."
- "The risk of inaction is..."

**Discussion papers:**
- "Management seeks the board's guidance on..."
- "We would value directors' views on..."
- "The key question for the board is..."
- "Options under consideration include..."

**Noting papers:**
- "The board is asked to note..."
- "Key points for the board's awareness..."
- "No action is required at this time"
- "This will return to the board in [month] for decision"

**Assurance papers:**
- "Assurance can be provided that..."
- "Management notes the following gap..."
- "Controls are operating effectively with the exception of..."

### Phrases to Avoid
- Vague: "various", "some", "a number of" — be specific
- Weasel words: "it is felt that", "it could be argued"
- Excessive hedging: "it might be worth considering" — recommend or don't
- Buzzwords: "synergies", "leverage", "paradigm shift"
- Double negatives: "not unlikely" — say "likely"
- Bureaucratic: "in respect of", "with regard to", "pursuant to"

## The QDI Principle (Question-Driven Insight)

Apply Board Intelligence's QDI methodology to every paper:

### Focus on What Matters
- Ask: "What is the ONE thing the board needs to take away from this paper?"
- Every section should answer "so what does this mean for the board?"
- Strip out everything that doesn't serve the board's decision or understanding

### Critical Thinking
- Seek out difficult questions that surface uncomfortable truths
- Present a balanced picture — not just the good news
- Include the risk of inaction alongside the risk of acting
- Show your working — don't just assert, demonstrate

### Communicate Clearly
- Lead with insight, not data
- "Revenue grew 12% driven by the new enterprise tier" not just "Revenue: $X.Xm"
- Use dashboards and visuals for complex data
- Every paper highlights the "so what?" and the action proposed

**Key QDI tenet: "Don't tell me everything you know. Tell me what I need to know."**

## The Inverted Pyramid

Structure every paper so the most important information comes first:

1. **The Lead** — Resolution/recommendation, the "so what" (page 1)
2. **Supporting Detail** — Evidence, analysis, options, risk (pages 2-3)
3. **Background and Reference** — Context, prior decisions, appendices (pages 3-5 + appendices)

If a director stops reading after page 1, they should still have the essential message.

## Visual Elements

Board papers benefit from visual elements in specific places. Flag opportunities for:

- **Dashboards** — For performance/metrics sections with multiple KPIs
- **Risk matrix** — For risk analysis (likelihood × impact grid)
- **Financial tables** — For financial implications with budget comparisons
- **Timeline/Gantt** — For implementation plans or phased approaches
- **Process diagrams** — For workflow changes or approval chains
- **Comparison tables** — For options analysis (features/pros/cons matrix)
- **Traffic light status** — For ongoing project or compliance reporting

Use this placeholder format when the user hasn't provided visuals:

```
> 📊 **[VISUAL PLACEHOLDER]** — {Description}
> *Suggested format: {chart type}*
> *Data needed: {what data would populate this}*
```

## Quality Checklist

Run this checklist before finalising any paper:

### Structure
- [ ] Cover sheet with paper type, sponsor, presenter, resolution (if applicable)
- [ ] Executive summary — 4-5 lines maximum, stands alone
- [ ] Background — 1 page maximum, details in appendices
- [ ] Recommendation — 1 page maximum, with rationale and alternatives
- [ ] All "issues" subsections — half page maximum each
- [ ] Total length — 3-5 pages excluding appendices

### Content
- [ ] Strategic alignment explained
- [ ] Financial implications summarised (in AUD)
- [ ] Risk analysis with treated and untreated levels
- [ ] Risk of inaction included
- [ ] Governance/regulatory compliance addressed
- [ ] Management responsibility assigned
- [ ] Prior approvals and stakeholder views noted

### Language
- [ ] Active voice throughout (passive only where justified)
- [ ] Plain English — no unexplained jargon or acronyms
- [ ] Sentences average 15-20 words
- [ ] Tone matches paper type
- [ ] "So what?" answered in every section
- [ ] No weasel words, hedging, or buzzwords

### Formatting
- [ ] Visual placeholders or actual visuals where data is complex
- [ ] Paper is self-contained (no verbal explanation needed)
- [ ] Appendices clearly labelled and referenced

## Common Anti-Patterns to Flag

When reviewing or drafting, proactively catch and fix these:

| Anti-Pattern | Problem | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Data dump | Pages of tables with no narrative | Lead with insight, use data as evidence |
| Management report repackage | Operational detail with a cover sheet | Rewrite for governance audience, focus on exceptions |
| Missing "so what" | Information without interpretation | Every section answers "so what does this mean?" |
| Buried lead | Key recommendation on page 4 | Inverted pyramid — most important first |
| Presentation deck | Slides implying verbal walkthrough | Papers must be self-contained |
| Risk whitewashing | Only upside presented | Transparent risk with treated and untreated levels |
| The endless paper | 15+ pages of dense text | 3-5 pages main body, everything else in appendices |
